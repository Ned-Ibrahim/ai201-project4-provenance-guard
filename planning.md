# Provenance Guard: Planning Document

Written before implementation. This is the spec that all code implements against, and the source material for AI prompting in Milestones 3–5.

## Problem Statement

Creative sharing platforms need a way to tell audiences whether a submitted piece of writing was made by the person posting it or generated by AI.
The goal is not to police creativity.
It is to protect attribution, build trust, and give readers honest context.
Perfect detection is impossible, so the system must communicate uncertainty honestly and give creators a path to contest a verdict.

A core design constraint: on a writing platform, a false positive (calling a human writer's work AI-generated) is worse than a false negative.
An unfair AI label can damage a real writer's reputation.
This asymmetry shapes the thresholds, the label wording, and the appeals design below.

## 1. Detection Signals

Two signals, deliberately independent: one semantic, one structural.

### Signal 1: LLM classification (Groq, llama-3.3-70b-versatile)

- **What it measures:** holistic semantic and stylistic coherence. The model reads the text and assesses whether it reads like AI output: hedged both-sides framing, stock transitions ("furthermore", "it is important to note"), evenly balanced paragraph rhythm, absence of lived specificity.
- **Why it differs between human and AI writing:** LLMs are trained on enormous amounts of AI and human text and can pick up on distributional patterns a hand-written heuristic cannot enumerate.
- **Output shape:** JSON from the model, parsed into a float `llm_score` in [0, 1], where 1.0 = confidently AI-generated, plus a short `rationale` string. Temperature 0 for repeatability.
- **Blind spot:** it judges *style*, not provenance. A human who writes in a formal, balanced register (academic writing, non-native speakers taught formal English) looks AI-ish to it. Lightly edited AI output with a few injected typos can fool it in the other direction. It is also nondeterministic at the margins and can fail (API down), so the pipeline must degrade gracefully.

### Signal 2: Stylometric heuristics (pure Python)

- **What it measures:** statistical texture of the text, via three metrics:
  1. **Sentence-length burstiness**: coefficient of variation of sentence lengths. Human writing mixes short punchy sentences with long ones; AI output tends toward uniform mid-length sentences.
  2. **Type-token ratio (TTR)**: unique words / total words. Counterintuitively, AI text often has *higher* local lexical variety but lower repetition of idiosyncratic pet words; very low TTR (heavy repetition) is a human signature (or a deliberate poetic device, see edge cases).
  3. **Punctuation and casing irregularity**: density of informal markers (contractions, ellipses, ALL CAPS words, missing sentence-initial capitals, "?!"). AI defaults to clean, standard punctuation.
- **Why it differs:** these are byproducts of how LLMs sample tokens (regression toward the typical) versus how humans actually type (idiosyncratic, uneven, sloppy in personal registers).
- **Output shape:** each metric normalized to [0, 1] where 1.0 = AI-like, averaged into `stylo_score` in [0, 1], with the per-metric values kept for the audit log.
- **Blind spot:** it judges *surface texture* only. Polished, professionally edited human prose (a journalist's final draft) scores AI-like. Deliberately constrained forms (haiku, minimalist poetry, legal writing) break the burstiness assumption. Very short texts make all three metrics statistically meaningless, so the API rejects texts under 80 characters rather than pretend.

### Why this pair

The two signals fail in different directions.
The LLM sees meaning but can be style-biased; stylometrics see texture but no meaning.
When they agree, confidence is justified.
When they disagree, that disagreement is itself information: it means the text sits in the ambiguous zone, and the system should say so rather than pick a side.

## 2. Uncertainty Representation

The score is a design decision before a technical one.
**Definition:** the system's `confidence` is a calibrated probability-like score in [0, 1] answering "how likely is it that this text is AI-generated?"

- `0.5` means "the evidence is genuinely balanced; we do not know."
- `0.6` means "slightly more AI-like than not, nowhere near enough to put a public AI label on someone's work."
- Only sustained agreement of both signals can push the score toward the extremes.

### Combination formula

```
raw = 0.65 * llm_score + 0.35 * stylo_score
disagreement = abs(llm_score - stylo_score)
if disagreement > 0.35:
    confidence = raw + (0.5 - raw) * min(1.0, (disagreement - 0.35) / 0.45)
else:
    confidence = raw
```

- LLM gets 65% weight because it is the richer signal; stylometrics get 35% as a structural check.
- **Disagreement damping** is the uncertainty mechanism: when the signals diverge by more than 0.35, the combined score is pulled proportionally toward 0.5. Two conflicting signals must never yield a confident verdict. This is what makes 0.51 and 0.95 *mean* different things: 0.95 requires both signals independently near-certain.
- If the LLM signal fails (API error), the system does not fall back to stylometrics alone pretending nothing happened: confidence is computed from stylometrics but hard-clamped into [0.35, 0.65] (the uncertain band), and the audit log records the degradation.

### Thresholds (asymmetric by design)

| Confidence range | Attribution | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| `>= 0.75` | `likely_ai` | High bar: a public AI label needs strong agreeing evidence, because a false positive harms a real writer. |
| `(0.40, 0.75)` | `uncertain` | Wide band, deliberately. Most contested content should land here. |
| `<= 0.40` | `likely_human` | Lower bar than the AI side: wrongly calling something human-made is the cheaper error on a writing platform. |

The uncertain band is asymmetric around 0.5 (0.40–0.75, not 0.40–0.60): the system needs *more* evidence to accuse than to clear.
This is the false-positive asymmetry made concrete.

### How we will test that scores are meaningful

Submit at least 4 curated inputs spanning the range (clearly AI, clearly human, formal-human borderline, edited-AI borderline) and check that (a) scores differ noticeably between the clear cases, (b) borderline cases land in or near the uncertain band, and (c) both signal scores are individually inspectable in the audit log when the combined score surprises us.

## 3. Transparency Label Design

Three variants, exact text fixed here before implementation.
Principles: plain language, no percentages presented as precise fact, never a flat accusation, always name the appeal path on an AI verdict.

**High-confidence AI (`likely_ai`, confidence >= 0.75):**

> **Likely AI-generated.** Our automated analysis found strong signs that this piece was written by an AI tool rather than a person. This is an automated assessment and can be wrong. If you are the creator and you wrote this yourself, you can appeal this label and a person will review it.

**Uncertain (`uncertain`, 0.40 < confidence < 0.75):**

> **Origin unclear.** Our automated analysis couldn't confidently determine whether this piece was written by a person or by an AI tool. Some signals point each way. Please read with your own judgment: this label reflects genuine uncertainty, not an accusation.

**High-confidence human (`likely_human`, confidence <= 0.40):**

> **Likely human-written.** Our automated analysis found strong signs that this piece was written by a person. No automated check is perfect, but nothing here suggests AI generation.

Note the deliberate asymmetries: the AI label leads with "automated" and "can be wrong" and names the appeal path in the label itself; the human label carries no appeal language (nobody appeals being called human); the uncertain label explicitly disclaims accusation.

## 4. Appeals Workflow

- **Who can appeal:** the creator of the content (identified by `creator_id`; in a real platform this would be an authenticated session; here the ID must match the submitting creator).
- **What they provide:** `content_id` and `creator_reasoning` (free text, required, non-empty, since the human reviewer needs something to work with).
- **What the system does on receipt:**
  1. Validates the `content_id` exists and the `creator_id` matches the original submitter.
  2. Rejects duplicate appeals for the same content (one open appeal at a time).
  3. Updates the content's status from `classified` to `under_review`.
  4. Writes an `appeal` event to the audit log that carries the creator's reasoning *and* a snapshot of the original decision (attribution, confidence, both signal scores) so a reviewer sees the full picture in one entry.
  5. Returns a confirmation with the new status.
- **What a human reviewer would see:** the appeal queue is every log entry with `event: "appeal"`; each contains the original verdict, both signal scores (so the reviewer can see *which* signal drove the verdict), the label the reader saw, and the creator's own words.
- Automated re-classification is explicitly out of scope.

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases

1. **Repetitive minimalist poetry.** A poem built on anaphora ("I have eaten / the plums / that were in / the icebox") has uniform short lines, tiny vocabulary, and clean punctuation, so stylometrics will read it as AI-like even though repetition-as-device is deeply human. Mitigation: the disagreement damper (the LLM usually recognizes poetic intent), plus the wide uncertain band; but a poem both signals misread will be misclassified, which is exactly what appeals are for.
2. **Formal prose by non-native English speakers.** Writers taught formal register produce balanced, hedged, standard-punctuation prose. Both signals lean AI on this, and they *agree*, so damping does not rescue it. This is the system's most likely unfair false positive. The high 0.75 accusation threshold exists mostly for this case, and the appeal template in testing uses exactly this scenario.
3. **Very short texts.** Three sentences give stylometrics nothing (variance of 3 numbers is noise). Mitigation: reject submissions under 80 characters with a clear 400 error instead of returning junk confidence.
4. **Human-edited AI drafts.** A writer who generates a draft and rewrites a third of it produces genuinely mixed provenance. The system has no concept of "partially AI"; the honest outcome is the uncertain band, and the borderline test inputs check that.

## Architecture

### Components

- **`app.py`**: Flask app: routes, request validation, rate limiting (Flask-Limiter, in-memory storage), response assembly.
- **`signals.py`**: `llm_signal(text)` and `stylometric_signal(text)`, each independently callable and testable.
- **`scoring.py`**: `combine_signals(llm, stylo)` implementing the formula above; `attribution_for(confidence)`; `label_for(attribution)` returning the exact label text.
- **`storage.py`**: SQLite (`provenance.db`): `contents` table (current state per submission) and `audit_log` table (append-only event log). Helpers: `save_submission`, `get_content`, `mark_under_review`, `append_log`, `recent_log`.

**Narrative:** a submission enters through `POST /submit`, passes validation and rate limiting, fans out to the two independent signals, and their scores are combined with disagreement damping into one confidence value that selects the attribution and the exact label text; the full decision (both signal scores included) is written to the SQLite audit log before the response returns.
An appeal enters through `POST /appeal`, is checked against the stored original decision, flips the content's status to `under_review`, and appends an appeal event carrying both the creator's reasoning and a snapshot of the decision being contested.

### API surface

| Endpoint | In | Out |
|---|---|---|
| `POST /submit` | `{text, creator_id}` | `{content_id, attribution, confidence, signals: {llm, stylometric}, label, status}` |
| `POST /appeal` | `{content_id, creator_id, creator_reasoning}` | `{content_id, status: "under_review", message}` |
| `GET /log` | `?limit=N` (default 20) | `{entries: [...]}` newest first |
| `GET /content/<id>` | (none) | current stored state incl. appeal if any |

### Rate limits

- `POST /submit`: **10/minute and 100/day per IP.** A real writer submits finished pieces, a handful a day at most; 10/minute leaves room for a workshop session or resubmission after edits, while making bulk-probing (an adversary tuning text against the detector) slow. 100/day caps sustained scripted abuse.
- `POST /appeal`: **5/minute and 20/day per IP.** Appeals are rarer than submissions and each one creates human review work; flooding the queue is the attack to prevent.
- `GET /log`, `GET /content`: exempt (read-only, grading/demo visibility; would be auth-gated in production).
